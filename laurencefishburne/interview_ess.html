A Man Called Fish

The pensive man moving about the suite at the St. James's Hotel on West Hollywood's Sunset Boulevard seems older than his 33 years. There is an air of detachment, a vulnerable wariness. He moves with feline grace, easy and languid, but his piercing, hooded eyes have the kind of haunting depth that evokes some unspeakable sadness reminiscent of a Langston Hughes riff: "I've known rivers. My soul has grown deep like the river."

Laurence Fishburne seems to tap a spirit source, deep and primal, to summon the smoldering energy that marks his extraordinary work as an actor. Yet while he has become famous for the explosive range of emotions he can bring to a role, in person he is low-key to the point of seeming almost depressed -- at once intense and cool, elusive and direct -- a brooding spirit for whom tragedy may be his true métier as an actor.

Fishburne, has been acting for more than 20 years, but it wasn't until last year, with his chilling portrayal of Ike Turner in the hit film What's Love Got to Do With It, that his reputation as a versatile character actor was elevated to that of a blazing star. Before then he was the guy people would pass on the street and vaguely recognize with a "Say, didn't I see you in uhh, uhh . "

 

He has been seen everywhere, it seems -- in movies, on TV, on Broadway. And he has inevitably been memorable, quietly racking up a Tony in 1992 for his lead role in August Wilson's Broadway hit Two Trains Running and an Emmy in 1993 for his guest appearance on Fox Broadcasting Co.'s Tribeca. Last year he was also nominated for an Oscar for Best Actor for his role in What's Love Got to Do With It. That makes him the only Black person to get top recognition in television, film and live drama. He is now starring in the John Singleton film Higher Learning, which is scheduled to open next year.

If Fishburne is one of those durable actors who seem always to get work, success has had its price. He contends that one of the reasons he's still around is that as a child actor he never had a television series that brought him before the American public every week to either typecast him or burn him out and make him expendable by adolescence.

Even so, the once exuberant, live-wire child actor has grown up to be a guarded, intensely private man, as slippery during an interview as a hooked fish out of water gasping in the air. This is a quality that makes Fishburne both intimidating and irresistible.
