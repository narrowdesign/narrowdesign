On this hot day in Los Angeles, in an old-style hotel done in slick 1940's Art Deco Hollywood glamour, Fishburne is seated on the couch in his suite, dressed more for the cold than the heat in a beige long-sleeve turtleneck sweater, matching pants and a suede vest. Just over six feet, he appears taller on-screen than in person, but he also has a rangy bigness about him -- solid and muscular -- that is a striking counterpoint to his casual elegance.

Fishburne seems surprised that some people find him "intense" and "brooding" -- an old spirit in a young man's eyes. Of course he's not any of those things, he insists. What is he then? "An actor," he coolly replies. "I'm really very warm, very loving . . ." His voice trails off; he lets out a small smile, then sinks back into the sofa, going into characteristic shutdown.

As an actor, Laurence Fishburne is not a classic pretty boy by Hollywood standards. He has too much of the look and the weight of the world etched in his face -- the high, sculpted forehead of the African; the slanting, inscrutable eyes of the Asian; the rising cheekbones of the Indian; and the long, strong jaw line of the European. The mix, however, has produced an exotically handsome Black man whose depth of feeling -- intelligence, strength, power and sheer visceral talent -- has made him a hit at the box office.

 

This comes as no surprise to those of us who remember his acting debut 22 years ago on a television special called If You Give a Dance, You Have to Pay the Band. He played a cocky, street-smart kid, and even then, at age 11, he was something. A natural. He made his film debut the next year in Cornbread, Earl and Me and then spent three years, from 1973 to 1976, on One Life to Live playing the troubled adopted son of characters portrayed by Al Freeman, Jr., and Ellen Holly, the first Black family (albeit dysfunctional) to be featured in a recurring role on a TV soap opera. Agnes Nixon, the soap's creator, reportedly saw Fishburne in the special and said, "Get me this kid. I need this kid on my show."

Fishburne contends he sort of fell into acting, though he believes from the moment of his first performance -- in a school play at age 10, which led to his auditioning for the special -- he was called to act. "I like to think that acting picked me," he says. "I didn't really pick acting. I wanted to be a basketball player. I wanted to be a doctor. I can't really say why I act. I just do. I always have. Why does Michael Jordan play basketball the way he does? Because he can."
