Like Jordan, Laurence Fishburne is naturally gifted. He has no formal training as an actor and no college education, yet he brings an erudite intelligence to every role and the enormous capacity to transform himself in any given part with consummate deftness. Whether he's playing the teenage navy boat gunner in Apocalypse Now or the chess whiz in Searching for Bobby Fischer or the cynical undercover cop in Deep Cover or the self-righteous father in Boyz N the Hood, Fishburne conveys an intensity, passion and complexity that get to the essence of the Black man.

These are the very qualities he brought to the role of Ike Turner, the man who married Tina Turner, made her a star and then terrorized her for 16 years and could have easily been demonized in a film documenting their story. "What I did was try to bring some kind of humanity and sensitivity to the cat. You know what I mean?" Fishburne asks with some of the same sympathy that marked his performance.

We do. For in Hollywood, where images are still overwhelmingly White, male and often racist, it is rare to see our stories told with anything approaching full-blown dimension. "I've been as affected by it as anyone else," Fishburne says of the racism that runs throughout his industry. "Look, the most recognition I have ever gotten, and will probably ever get, came from playing a wife-beating, coke-snorting madman. Everybody said, 'That's great.' I tried to get around it, but I didn't escape it."

Fishburne believes that Sidney Poiter and Denzel Washington are two Black actors who have escaped much of this negative stereotyping. "If you look at their work, the images they present, they are always very clean-cut, very righteous guys. Guys who always do the right thing -- are very responsible. They're dignified. And it's wonderful that they've managed to do that. How they've managed to do that, I wish I knew."

What Fishburne has managed to do is get work as an actor for most of his life. And since the great success of his Ike incarnation, he is now in demand more than ever. In addition to Higher Learning, he's starring opposite Ellen Barkin in Bad Company, a CIA thriller due for release early next year, and Just Cause, a murder mystery that teams him with Sean Connery and Blair Underwood. He's also working with novelist Gloria Naylor on a film treatment of her book Mama Day.

"Laurence will be the quintessential George Andrews," Naylor says of the main character in her book. "He's every woman's definition of love. A man who understands that what a woman basically needs is to be loved unconditionally."

In real life, however, Fishburne says he hasn't a clue to understanding women. "What I've learned is that it's okay for me to understand that men and women are different," he explains. "And that it's okay if you don't understand everything that makes a woman tick. That's fine. You don't have to understand, because if you try to understand it will just make you crazy. Just understanding we are different is enough."
