The woman now in Fishburne's life is actress Victoria Dilliard, the stunning Black beauty who starred opposite him in Deep Cover. They met on the set. Fishburne was estranged from his wife at the time and is now divorced, though this is a part of his life he refuses to discuss. In fact, there are great areas of his personal life that have clearly caused him deep pain -- areas he refuses to talk about on the record. Off the record he confesses to some of the hurt, explains why he won't go public with it and then pleads that his confidence not be betrayed. Of his romance with Dilliard he will only say: "I love her very much." Of his two children he does say: "I don't like to talk about my kinds because they are going to have a hard enough time as it is. To begin with, their daddy's famous, and he doesn't live with them. I just try to love them a lot. I just try to love them as hard as I can and as much as possible."

An only child raised by a single mother, Fishburne says he spent his own childhood "hanging around with older people. I had to invent things on my own," he says. "And I learned things on my own." The downside to being a child actor is that you forfeit a childhood. And this burden of having to grow up too soon is perhaps what gives Fishburne is older-soul quality, his trace of melancholy. "I wasn't on the swimming team, the track team or the basketball team. I didn't go to the prom. I didn't have dates," he says of a life that lacked the typical rites of passage that takes boys to men. As a result, the child actor who grew up to be a movie star comes across as mature yet shy, grounded by vulnerable, seductive yet unwise to the ways of women.

Fishburne was born in Brooklyn, where he attended grade school. Just as he was set to enter New York's prestigious High School of Performing Arts, he landed the role of the gunner in Apocalypse Now and found himself, at age 14, in the jungles of the Philippines, where he spent the next year and a half working on Francis Ford Coppola's grueling film about the war in Vietnam. It was there, surrounded by White males -- Marlon Brando, Robert Duvall, Dennis Hopper, Martin Sheen -- big egos and machismo, that "Laurence," as he was then known, got reduced to "Larry." Good friends just call him Fish.

"I was called Laurence before I did Apocalypse Now," Fishburne notes, commenting on the flap that greeted his insistence last year that he be called Laurence. He was not putting on airs or suddenly becoming difficult; he was simply reclaiming his name.

Of course, in a larger sense, he was also staking his claim to power, something that puts him in the company of the few other brothers who are flexing muscle in Hollywood. "We have a genuine love for one another," he says of men such as Wesley Snipes, Denzel Washington, John Singleton and Spike Lee -- the Black men who now carry box-office clout. "We all know one another and support one another. If we don't, who will?"

These days, with his star power still rising, Fishburne's choice of roles is motivated by "whether or not I'm going to have a good time doing it, whether I'm going to be able to apply some of my creative juice to it, whether or not I'm going to make a difference. There's a lot of stuff that's being offered, and not all of it is great."
