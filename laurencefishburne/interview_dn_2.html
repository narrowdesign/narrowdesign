Fishburne's last two New York stage projects were extremely successful. Four years ago, he enjoyed a triumph Off-Broadway with a play that he wrote, directed and co-starred in. Riff Raff, about two druggies in an apartment after they have killed another person, has been adapted into a movie, Once in the Life, which Fishburne directed; it will be released in September. Three years earlier, he won a Tony for his portrayal of a recently released convict in August Wilson's Two Trains Running.

"F. Murray Abraham put it best," says Fishburne. " 'Theater is church for actors.' This is where I come to do my service to my craft. This is not about me."

With that, he produces a deep rumble of a laugh.

Even if this play isn't about his ego, there are intriguing parallels between Henry II and Lawrence Fishburne III. "I'm divorced," he says. "I have three children: two sons and a daughter. We've been apart for seven years. So the family dynamic is identical."

Again, a laugh comes out from the center of the Earth.

The similarities between Fishburne's life and art came home to him after a dress rehearsal attended by his kids and ex-wife. "My first son looked at me and said, 'It's our family in another century.' "

Speaking of the distant past, Fishburne says his performance has been deepened by his first past life. "I'm from Scotland. I know what I was, but I won't tell you," he says with a mischievous expression. "Some things are better left unsaid. It's something that good."

 

In the more recent past, Fishburne grew up in Park Slope, the only child of a teacher and a correction officer. He began acting at age 10, was cast in the movie Apocalypse Now at 14, played a wacky cowboy on the TV show Pee-wee's Playhouse and went on to character roles in movies like The Color Purple and School Daze.

With Boyz N the Hood in 1991, Fishburne leaped to prominence with as a young father coping with a teenage son in a gang-infested L.A. ghetto. Since then he has had an astonishing variety of movie roles. In 1993, he was nominated for an Oscar for playing Ike Turner in What's Love Got to Do With It, and in 1995, he did Othello, becoming the first African-American to play the Moor on film.

"After Othello," says Fishburne, "it was, like, 'I can stop acting. I have played one of the great characters in the English language. I feel I have played him well and honorably. I have nothing to prove any more.' It was like Michael Jordan when he walked away from basketball the first time."

There was only one problem. "I was only 33, so it was a little early for retirement."

Lucky for Hollywood and Broadway and us.
