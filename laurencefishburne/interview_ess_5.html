One of the roles he turned down a few years ago was the Radio Raheem character in Spike Lee's Do the Right Thing. Though Fishburne had worked with Lee in the past in School Daze, he says he didn't like the script of Do the Right Thing. "Spike's a decent guy; he can be a swell guy, but we basically don't see eye to eye. He thinks his way. I think mine."

John Singleton, who directed him in Boyz N the Hood, is the brother Fishburne is down with. "I love John," he says simply. "And even if we don't agree, that's okay, you know. We are able to get past it and talk about it."

Fishburne has a crew of male friends he is close to, though this, too, is private. "Do I have to tell you?" he asks with boyish evasiveness, when requested to name names. "They're my close friends, which is why I don't want to say who they are." One good friend who is known is actor Martin Sheen, who worked with Fishburne in Apocalypse Now and became a helpful father figure. He is now the godfather of one of Fishburne's children.

If Fishburne isn't given to telling all, neither is he the self-absorbed star who spends a lot of time analyzing what he does as an actor. He says what he brings to every role is honesty. "It is the thing I like about myself the most. I'm honest. I try to be honest in my work as well as my life. And I'm smart. I have a natural gift for speaking and I have a good ear and I like to read. But I'm not so smart that I sit around examining what I do all that closely. I'm smart enough not to do that."

It is the honest Black male in him who says that his favorite role was not the Ike Turner character he played with such tragic heroism, or even the upcoming role he has with Ellen Barkin, in which he gets to do a couple of steamy love scenes. "No," he says, "my favorite is the character I played in King of New York. It's what I call a two-gun kid. A straight-up psychotic killer. You don't get to show up in movies and kill people at random and have fun doing it, and have people like you." Certainly not if you are a Black man in Hollywood. Unless, of course, you are Laurence Fishburne, and straight up know how to act.
