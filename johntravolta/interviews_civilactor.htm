Look who's talking about Boston's biggest movie of the year: John Travolta spills the beans about celebrity, cinema, Scientology, and his upcoming turn as controversial lawyer Jan Schlichtmann in A Civil Action.

A Civil Actor
by Lisa Gerson

John Travolta holds up a bright-red lobster by the tail and wonders aloud how to approach it. "Is this something we're supposed to eat?" he asks, grinning up at his personal chef. "Or is this just decor?" Assured that it is, in fact, just an empty shell for garnish, Travolta moves on to the spring rolls, which contain the missing lobster meat. The sun is shining on the exclusive Los Angeles suburb of Brentwood, filtered through wispy clouds that today obscure views of the Getty Museum, the airport, and the Pacific Ocean.

Travolta munches pensively, carefully choosing his words. On the subject of Jan Schlictmann, the Boston lawyer he plays in A Civil Action, which will be released nationwide next month, he tries to be diplomatic. "I tried to find something funny about the character," he says finally, between mouthfuls. "At least, the way it was written was kind of-I want to say this the right way so I don't get in any trouble over it-but I guess I found the way [the character] was written was mildly arrogant and insensitive. So I play up to that. I play him flawed."

Which may be a fairly accurate reading of the real Jan Schlichtmann, the decidedly unheroic protagonist of Jonathan Harr's best-selling book, on which the movie is based. (See "A Civil Action Hero," by Gretchen Voss, Boston Magazine, September 1998.) The real Schlichtmann was a young hotshot lawyer who was beginning to make a name in downtown legal circles when an amazing case fell into his lap. A dozen children in Woburn had died of leukemia over a 15-year period, and foul water-contaminated with carcinogens dumped by local factories-was suspected. It was a bear of a case, and Schlichtmann couldn't let it go, despite warnings from colleagues that it was "a black hole" that would consume him.
