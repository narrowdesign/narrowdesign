STRIPE IN-PROGRESS TECHNIQUES OFFICE

Hillsborough, N.C. January 25, 2024

FROM: N. A. L. Jones

MEMORANDUM FOR: Members and Friends of the Infinite History of Technology Initiative

If you’re reading this it means you’re somebody I trust. Either we’re collaborators already or we should be. Since we’re on the web here, somebody else might’ve sent you the link. In that case you can stop reading. But I suspect you won’t. Because if I’m any good at this, you’re curious where this is going. So am I. I’ll start by admitting that what “this” is, isn’t clear. Just need to get some thoughts out, to give an idea some critical eyes and a chance.

What’s compelling me is that few in our field (“Tech”, “The Web”, etc.) know our origin stories. Ask someone who the pioneers of computing are and you’ll hear “Jobs” and “Gates”. If they’ve done a little studying, maybe “Woz” or “Turing”. But Licklider, Shannon, Sutherland, McCarthy, Kay, or Engelbart are unlikely to come up. The names MIT, Bell Labs, Stanford, and PARC are well known but the conditions that made it possible for people to turn playful discoveries into transformative technologies in those institutions are not.

Like my contemporaries, I didn’t know a Difference Engine from a Dynabook until, during my Stripe onboarding in 2017, I pulled a thick yellow paperback off the shelf at the old Berry St. office. For the 7 years since, I’ve followed the trails The Dream Machine sent me down. The details of the meandering map of our industry’s origins are still as low-res as its boundaries.

The project being proposed here is to throw this map into stark relief on the Web. The high-res version includes dozens of institutions, hundreds of breakthrough ideas, thousands of people, and countless connections between them all. I’m in it. So are you. The company we work for and the ones we’ll start together 10 years from now are in it too.

We work at a once-in-a-generation company that sometimes feels like it’s just getting started. Whether that proves true or not will depend on how we spend our time here and whether or not we create the conditions necessary for our best work to occur. Even when you zoom way out to see the big picture, Stripe is legible and spreading rapidly. In order to see that picture clearly, and our place in it, we have to build the map.

My medium is the web, but my preferred medium for learning is the Book. Several great books tell the tales of personal computing, AI, computer graphics, and the Internet. We’ve published a few of them and I hope we do more! I only know the stories because these books exist. What doesn’t exist is a website where you can explore the origins of these foundational technologies and how they led to this moment. I am writing this into a website in North Carolina and it’s rendering what I typed (assuming you’re still with me) on your screen someplace else. That’s magical but wasn’t inevitable.

This is starting to feel long. Conventional wisdom says nobody reads marketing copy yet this is a pitch and here you are. An unfolding story is hard to look away from. The larger story we’re characters in isn’t the one unfolding in popular culture or the industry press. It has the potential to take a very different shape if we understand our role in it.

What exists because we did? What won’t if we don’t spend our collective time on it? Did you know that without the University of Utah, there would be no Pixar, Atari, Adobe, Netscape, or Chuck E. Cheese? Not that 3d animation, video games, computer graphics, web browsers, and bad pizza wouldn’t have happened anyway but they happened because Ivan Sutherland went from ARPA to Utah. Like all historical accounts that’s an oversimplification but practically nobody knows that! If you already did (or just learned something) we share an obligation to use our talents to tell others.

My medium is the web. Maybe yours is posters, data visualization, writing stories, or giving talks. Whatever it is, the history of technology offers infinite inspiration, content, and no existing platform. I want to make sense of this big, messy, wonderful, terrible industry we’ve dedicated our careers to. The opportunity to make meaningful progress toward a better version of it is electrifying. I want to understand my place in it and help the generations who’ll follow to see theirs. They’ll get to judge our current efforts one day. They’ll follow our trails and tell our stories, if they’re any good!

I have a lot more to say but will save it for when we next meet. Hopefully, the discussion may be a little less rambling than this effort that I am now completing.
